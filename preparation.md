# 1. Preparation for the hiking trip

## 1.1. Route Planning

**Data Matrix:**
*   **Target:** Karnizny Peak (Summit Elevation: 4075 m)
*   **Location:** Ili Alatau range, Tien Shan mountains, near Almaty, Kazakhstan
*   **Access Route:** Ayusai Gorge up to the Space Station (Cosmostation)
*   **Estimated Trek Distance:** ~27-30 km total traverse
*   **Standard Route Window:** 2 days minimum for safe acclimatization and execution

The mathematical blueprint of the route was clean on my screen. I plotted the trail using GPS maps, tracking the elevation gain from the base up past the Cosmostation to the ridges connecting Tourist Peak, Turan Peak, and ultimately Karnizny Peak. On paper, it was a sequence of manageable waypoints, predictable inclines, and calculable pacing. 

> **[Confession]:** I tried to calculate and predict every single meter in my route, but it is almost impossible.

---

## 1.2. The Failed Attempts

Every mountain has a way of testing a climber's intent before the real trial begins. This was not my first look at Karnizny.

*   **Attempt 1:** Halted by unpredicted early-season snow tracking that obscured the trail indicators entirely, forcing a tactical retreat back toward the lower valleys.
*   **Attempt 2:** Blocked by logistics and unyielding gear failures that made a summit push mathematically impossible within the allocated weekend timeline.

> **[Confession]:** A rational mindset looks at repeated failures or early retreats as a clear data pattern—a signal that the mountain is presenting complex, unpredictable conditions. But my pride flipped the narrative. I didn't see the failed attempts as a warning; I saw them as an insult. The frustration of turning back twice created a toxic determination inside me. I wasn't climbing to appreciate the high alpine anymore; I was climbing to settle a score.

---

## 1.3. A Risky Venture: Going Solo

The final decision was made: I would go completely alone. No partner, no backup team, no shared tracking. Just one person against a 3,900-meter peak.

> **[Confession]:** Going solo in the high peaks is a serious undertaking that demands absolute humility and flawless contingency planning. I convinced myself I was going solo because I wanted the pure, uninterrupted challenge of a solitary alpine experience. The truth was far less noble. I went solo because I was too impatient to wait for anyone else’s schedule to align with mine. I was rushing, driven by the urge to check this summit off my list, willingly trading the irreplaceable safety safety-net of a partner for the immediate satisfaction of executing my plan on my own terms.
